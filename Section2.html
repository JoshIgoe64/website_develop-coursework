2.1.5 Describing copyright and other constraints affecting websites
  • Many uses of images online may not fall under fair use as they are being used to sell a monetised product; not in education, as criticism, parody or any other exception. Many, many things fall under intellectual property, which cannot be used without the users permission.
  • I will be aware of this when creating my website by avoiding watermarked or copyrighted images when selecting images. Aspect ratios on different devices also make website developing far harder than they could be, as the graphics and I need to accomodate for all screen sizes; landscape, portrait, laptop, phone, TV etc.

2.1.6 Describing access issues that need taking into account - describe issues that people with disabilities may face when using web pages and how we can overcome them.
People with colour impairments, this can be combated by having multiple versions of a website for people that are different sorts of colourblind. Dyslexia can also be accommodated with simple fonts such as Sans Serif and dyslexic friendly colour schemes. Deaf people can also be accommodated for by having subtitles on videos to accomodate for lack of hearing. You also need to be aware of overstimulation for those with Autism, ADHD or have Seizures, and combat the dangers of this by keeping the website simple and not overwhelming.

2.1.7 Describe which are the appropriate file types for websites
HTML is the standard for the actual website file(HyperText Markup Language), and GIF, PNG and JPEG are the common file used to complement this in images gifs and videos. PNG is the most common file type on a website as this was designed specifically for websites. Javascript and Perl are also commonly used as scripts on a website to complement the HTML and CSS.
